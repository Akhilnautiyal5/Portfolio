A little Inspiration

Sonam Wangchuk

He is the man whose extraordinary life inspired the filmmakers of 3 Idiots to create the unforgettable character of Phunsukh Wangdu.

The 52-year-old engineer from Leh gained national attention in 1988 when he established a revolutionary school that welcomed children written off as failures by society. His goal was to make learning fun and practical, moving away from rote memorization.

Wangchuk's groundbreaking innovations, such as "Ice Stupas" and solar-heated mud huts, have earned him global recognition for finding sustainable solutions in challenging terrains.

“More than inspiration, it was empathy that led me to find solutions in areas that had been plaguing the lives of common people. Be it the water issue or education for children, these were people in need, and I was driven by this irresistible empathy to resolve their issues. A virtue that was instilled in me by my mother as well as my ancestors, empathy is what makes us all human, and that must come naturally. If we wish to see India as a great nation, what we must strive towards is to become great citizens first. Let’s start that by doing good for the society.”

Short Story

The clockmaker's Apprentice

In a quiet mountain village nestled between the pines, lived an old clockmaker named Mr. Harwin. Known for his intricate timepieces, Harwin was respected but solitary—his shop filled with ticking marvels and stories never told. One morning, a boy named Eli, with smudged glasses and an insatiable curiosity, knocked on his door.

“I want to learn how to fix time,” Eli said.
Harwin chuckled. “You mean build clocks?”
“No,” Eli replied. “Fix time. Make it count.”

Amused, Harwin accepted him as an apprentice. Day after day, Eli learned to carve gears, align springs, and listen—not just to the ticks of clocks, but to the rhythm of life. At first, his hands trembled. He broke more than he fixed. But Harwin never scolded him. “Precision grows from patience,” he’d say.



One snowy afternoon, Harwin fell ill. The village panicked—a large town bell had stopped, and it was tradition to ring it during the Winter Festival. “Without that bell,” a villager said, “the festival can’t begin.” Everyone turned to Eli.

Trembling, Eli entered Harwin’s workshop alone. He studied the bell’s mechanism. Remembering everything—the balance, the weight, the patience—he worked through the night. By dawn, his fingers were blistered, but the bell’s hammer was ready.

As the sun rose, the bell rang through the village. Cheers erupted. The festival began, brighter than ever.
Harwin, weak but smiling, whispered, “You didn’t just fix a bell. You fixed time—for everyone.”

Years later, Eli became the village’s clockmaker. But he taught more than mechanics. He taught children to see wonder in small gears, the value of quiet effort, and that even when time seems broken, it can be rebuilt—with hope and heart.
